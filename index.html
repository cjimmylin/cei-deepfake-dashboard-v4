Dimension Reference Guide (click to expand)

This table explains each of the 25 scoring dimensions used to analyze governance statements. Dimensions are grouped into six categories that trace the lifecycle of a governance response — from recognizing the harm through to systemic capacity-building. Each dimension is scored 0–100 via keyword matching with false-positive gating.

DimensionWhat It MeasuresWhy It Matters
Category A: Recognition & Definition (4 dimensions)
Deepfake MentionWhether the statement explicitly names deepfakes, face-swap technology, or synthetic intimate media — as opposed to vague references to "manipulated content."A governance text cannot address what it does not name. Explicit mention is the minimum threshold for relevance; without it, deepfake harms fall through regulatory gaps designed for other problems.
NCII RecognitionWhether nonconsensual intimate imagery (NCII) or image-based sexual abuse (IBSA) is recognized as a specific harm category, distinct from generic harassment or defamation.Treating NCII as ordinary defamation misses the unique psychology of sexual-image abuse — the permanence, the re-victimization cycle, and the gendered targeting. Specific recognition enables targeted remedies.
Gendered HarmWhether the statement acknowledges that nonconsensual deepfakes disproportionately target women and girls, rather than treating the harm as gender-neutral.Research consistently shows 80–95% of deepfake pornography targets women. Gender-neutral framing renders this structural pattern invisible and leads to governance responses that fail to protect the primary victims.
Dignity FramingWhether governance is grounded in human dignity, bodily autonomy, or personal integrity — rather than purely privacy-based or harm-reduction framing.Privacy framing treats the issue as information control; dignity framing recognizes that fabricating someone's sexual image violates their personhood. This distinction shapes whether remedies focus on data deletion or restorative justice.
Category B: Restriction & Prevention (5 dimensions)
App RegulationWhether creation tools themselves — face-swap apps, "nudify" services, AI model distribution — are regulated, not just the output they produce.Regulating only the output (the deepfake image) leaves the supply side untouched. App-level regulation can prevent harm at the source by restricting tools purpose-built for nonconsensual intimate imagery.
Safeguards RequiredWhether technical safeguards are mandated: watermarking, consent verification, content authentication (C2PA), or deepfake detection integration.Technical safeguards create friction in the abuse pipeline. Watermarking enables attribution; consent verification at generation time is more effective than post-hoc takedowns.
Platform ObligationsWhether hosting platforms have specific duties: content moderation, transparency reporting, duty of care, or liability for deepfake NCII.Platforms are the distribution bottleneck. Without clear obligations, takedown is voluntary and inconsistent — victims face different response times and standards across every platform.
Training DataWhether consent is required for using facial imagery in AI training datasets, with restrictions on scraping and dataset auditing.Every face-swap model is trained on real faces, often scraped without consent. Training data governance addresses the root input — if models cannot be trained on unconsented imagery, the creation pipeline narrows.
Age ProtectionWhether enhanced protections exist for children and minors — aggravated penalties, mandatory reporting, or integration with CSAM frameworks.Minors face compounded harm from deepfake abuse: developmental vulnerability, school-based distribution, and limited legal agency. Age-specific provisions close gaps that adult-focused legislation leaves open.
Category C: Victim Empowerment (5 dimensions)
Takedown MechanismWhether a right to removal exists with defined procedures, timelines, and single-point reporting for nonconsensual deepfake content.Speed is critical — every hour an image remains online increases its spread exponentially. Formal takedown mechanisms with enforceable timelines (e.g., 24–48 hours) transform removal from a favor into a right.
Notification RightWhether victims have a right to be notified when deepfake content using their likeness is detected on platforms.Most victims learn about deepfakes from third parties, often after widespread distribution. Proactive notification enables faster response and reduces the psychological ambush of discovery.
Civil RemediesWhether civil causes of action exist: damages, injunctive relief, attorney fees, or expedited proceedings for deepfake NCII victims.Criminal prosecution requires state action and high evidentiary standards. Civil remedies empower victims to act directly, pursue compensation, and obtain injunctions without depending on prosecutorial discretion.
Support ServicesWhether victim support is mandated or funded: helplines, psychological counseling, legal aid, and advocacy services.Legal rights without support infrastructure are hollow. Victims of image-based sexual abuse face severe psychological harm; trauma-informed support services bridge the gap between rights on paper and help in practice.
Reupload PreventionWhether re-upload is prevented after removal — hash-matching databases, URL blocking, search de-indexing, and ongoing monitoring.Takedown without reupload prevention is a game of whack-a-mole. Hash-matching (e.g., StopNCII, PhotoDNA) enables cross-platform blocking so that a single removal request propagates everywhere.
Category D: Prosecution & Accountability (5 dimensions)
Criminal OffenseWhether creation, distribution, or possession of nonconsensual deepfake intimate imagery is defined as a criminal offense.Criminalization sends a deterrence signal and provides a pathway for state-led prosecution. However, over-reliance on criminal law risks racialized enforcement — this tension is tracked in the dashboard's minority reports.
Penalty StructureWhether specific penalties are defined: imprisonment terms, fines, aggravating factors for minors or repeat offenders, and sentencing guidelines.A criminal offense without defined penalties is symbolic. Graduated penalties (higher for targeting minors, repeat offenses, or commercial distribution) calibrate deterrence to harm severity.
Distributor LiabilityWhether platforms and intermediaries bear liability for hosting or distributing deepfake NCII, and under what conditions (knowledge standard, safe harbor).Section 230 / E-Commerce Directive safe harbors were designed for neutral conduits. When platforms algorithmically recommend content, the conduit analogy breaks — liability frameworks must evolve accordingly.
Intent StandardWhether the law specifies mens rea: intent to harm, knowledge of non-consent, recklessness, or strict liability for deepfake offenses.The intent standard determines who can be prosecuted. Requiring "intent to harm" lets creators claim artistic or satirical purpose; strict liability may over-criminalize. Most effective frameworks use a knowledge-of-nonconsent standard.
Cross-BorderWhether cross-jurisdictional enforcement is addressed: mutual legal assistance, extraterritorial application, or harmonization agreements.Deepfakes are created in one jurisdiction, hosted in another, and consumed globally. Without cross-border mechanisms, perpetrators exploit jurisdictional gaps — hosting in permissive jurisdictions while targeting victims elsewhere.
Category E: Normative Foundations — Rights & Consent (3 dimensions)
Consent FrameworkWhether consent requirements exist for use of likeness in synthetic media — opt-in/opt-out models, purpose limitation, and withdrawal rights.Consent is the normative core: if a person did not consent to their likeness being used, the creation is wrongful regardless of intent. Frameworks range from narrow (explicit opt-in) to broad (right to own image in civil law traditions).
Human RightsWhether international human rights instruments (UDHR, ICCPR, ECHR, CEDAW) are cited as the normative basis for deepfake governance.Human rights grounding elevates governance from domestic policy preference to universal obligation. CEDAW provides gender-specific protections; ICCPR Article 17 (privacy) and ECHR Article 8 (private life) create enforceable rights frameworks.
Religious DignityWhether the statement frames deepfake harm through theological concepts: imago Dei (Christianity), karamah (Islam), tzelem Elohim (Judaism), or other faith-based dignity traditions.Religious dignity frames add moral weight from faith traditions that have shaped billions of people's understanding of personhood. They complement secular frameworks by grounding bodily integrity in metaphysical commitments that resist utilitarian trade-offs.
Category F: Systemic & Forward-Looking (3 dimensions)
Labeling/DisclosureWhether AI-generated or synthetic content must be labeled, disclosed, or marked with provenance metadata (e.g., EU AI Act Article 50).Labeling is upstream prevention: if synthetic content is identifiable, platforms can auto-flag it, users can assess credibility, and victims can prove fabrication. Without labels, authenticity becomes unfalsifiable.
ImplementationWhether enforcement authorities are designated, resourced, and given compliance mechanisms — bridging the gap between law-on-paper and law-in-practice.The best-written law is meaningless without enforcement capacity. This dimension captures whether governance statements assign authority (e.g., eSafety Commissioner), allocate budget, define compliance cycles, and include sunset review provisions.
Education/AwarenessWhether public education, media literacy, school curricula, or awareness campaigns about deepfake risks and victim reporting are promoted.Prevention through awareness complements legal deterrence. Media literacy helps potential victims recognize deepfakes; reporting awareness ensures people know where to seek help. Education scales where enforcement cannot.
